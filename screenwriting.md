# Screenwriting Crashcourse

## The Anatomy of the Idea

* **The Logline :** Identify your protagonist, their goal, and the "antagonist" (the obstacle). Use the formula: *When [Inciting Incident], a [Protagonist] must [Objective] or else [Stakes].*
* **The Three-Point Structure :** Don't overcomplicate. Map out three specific moments:
    1. **The Hook:** What breaks the status quo?
    2. **The Midpoint:** A major shift where the stakes double.
    3. **The Climax:** The final confrontation where the hero succeeds or fails.

## The Visual Language

* **Formatting 101 :** Learn the "Big Three": **Sluglines** (Where/When), **Action Lines** (What we see/hear), and **Dialogue** (What is said).
  * *Rule:* Never write what a character is "thinking"—only what they are *doing*.
* **The "In Late, Out Early" Rule :** We analyze a professional script page.
  * Start a scene at the last possible moment of tension and cut it the second the narrative point is made. This creates the "web-series" snap.

## The "Sprint" & Feedback

* **The 30-Minute Sprint:** Write your **Opening Scene** (approx. 2 pages). No overthinking, no "perfecting." Use a free tool like *WriterDuet* or *Celtx* to handle the margins.
* **The Cold Read :** Exchange scripts with a partner. Read theirs aloud to them.
  * **The Litmus Test:** If the reader pauses or gets confused, your action lines are too dense. If the dialogue feels "stiff," it’s likely too "on-the-nose."

## The "Single-Class" Toolkit

| Rule | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **White Space** | A script should be "airy." If a paragraph of action is longer than 4 lines, break it up. |
| **Show, Don't Tell** | Instead of a character saying "I'm sad," show them throwing a birthday cake in the trash. |
| **1 Page = 1 Minute** | For a 5-minute web episode, your target is exactly 5–6 pages. |

> **Pro Tip:** Your first draft will be bad—that’s its job. A script is a **technical manual** for a director, not a novel. Be clear, be brief, and be visual.
